Implementations and Performances

We have implemented a wavelet-based analysis and compared the results of this with a conventional FFT-based analysis. The temporal resolution, as measured by the half-width maximum, was significantly better for the continuous wavelet transform (CWT).

CWT mean (SD) 8.40 (8.82) ms
128-point FFT, 12.92 (9.70) ms
64-point FFT, 10.80 (5.69) ms
  • 1
    A total of 50 consecutive embolic signals from 5 patients with symptomatic carotid stenosis were used for analysis.
  • 2
    All had been recorded using a commercially available transcranial Doppler system with a 2MHz transducer.
  • 3
    The quadrature sound track data were then exported to a PC for signal analysis using a Matlab program.
  • 4
    The data were analyzed using both the FFT and the CWT.
  • 5
    For CWT analysis, a 64-scale WT using Morlet wavelet was applied to both forward and reverse signals, representing the time-scale distribution of the signals.
  • 6
    Results obtained for the 128-point FFT, the 64-point FFT, and the CWT were compared using paired t-tests.

The results obtained by the CWT heavily depend upon the choice of an appropriate wavelet. Using a more appropriate wavelet will result in higher amplitude wavelet coefficients and, hence, a higher EBR value. We selected the Morlet wavelet from those available in the Matlab signal-processing because it best described embolic signals.

However, it may be possible to construct a better wavelet that will more accurately describes such signals and result in a higher EBR. Nevertheless, this study with a standard wavelet transform suggests that the wavelet approach is well suited to analyze the short-duration embolic signals.
